## Design Rationale

**1. Progressive case integration**
The Creciendo Juntos narrative is introduced as a brief hook at Slide 5 (replacing in-class reading). Content from the case deepens with each topic block. The full narrative is assigned as pre-reading for Session 2.

**2. Exercise alignment with ToC construction**
Exercises follow the ToC construction sequence: (1) map the results chain → (2) trace the causal mechanism (COM-B) → (3) classify assumptions → (4) scan external influences → (5) derive monitoring priorities. Each exercise builds on the previous; outputs accumulate into a completed ToC.

**3. Narrative length and exercise transitions**
The in-session narrative read is replaced by a 1-paragraph vignette (Slide 5). Transitions between exercises are made explicit — each debrief slide names what was produced and how it connects to the next task. Exercise 4 is redesigned as a 10-min full-group facilitated discussion (saving ~10 min vs. small-group format).

**4. Bridge to Session 2 (Results Matrix)**
Slide 34 shows one populated Results Matrix row drawn from the day's ToC. The closing pre-reading assignment connects directly to Session 2. Participants end Session 1 knowing exactly what artifact they carry forward.

---

#### Slide 7 — What are results? Outcomes and impact
**⏱ 6 min**

**Purpose**
Introduce outcomes (changes in people, behaviors, systems) and impact (long-term population-level change). Distinguish both from outputs.

**Content on slide**
- **OUTCOME:** A change in knowledge, attitude, behavior, or condition of a person or system resulting from the intervention
- **IMPACT:** Long-term, higher-order change attributable (in part) to the intervention — often at population level
- In Creciendo Juntos: *Outcome = caregiver changes parenting behavior. Impact = child achieves age-appropriate development by age 3.*

**Key question for participants**
> *"The program delivers sessions (output). Sessions are supposed to change caregivers (outcome). Changed caregivers are supposed to develop children (impact). But HOW does each step happen?"*

---

#### Slide 8 — The results chain: a causal map of change
**⏱ 8 min**

**Purpose**
Introduce the results chain as the basic structure linking inputs to impact. Scaffold for the Theory of Change.

**Content on slide**

```
INPUTS → ACTIVITIES → OUTPUTS → OUTCOMES → IMPACT
```

*Creciendo Juntos populated at each level:*
- **INPUTS:** 750 facilitators · curriculum · USD 285M
- **ACTIVITIES:** Parenting sessions · home visits · health center visits
- **OUTPUTS:** 85,000 caregivers reached · 36 sessions per group per year
- **OUTCOMES:** Caregiver behavior change (interaction quality, nutrition practices)
- **IMPACT:** 65% of children achieve age-appropriate development by 24 months

**Facilitator note**
> Draw the chain on the board as you speak. Ask: *"Which of these boxes is easiest to measure? Which is hardest to attribute to the program?"* — sets up the contribution analysis thread.

---

#### Slide 9 — Direct and distal outcomes: why the distinction matters
**⏱ 6 min**

**Purpose**
Establish the key conceptual distinction that recurs all week. The program reaches one group to change outcomes in a different group.

**Content on slide**
- **DIRECT OUTCOME:** Change in the group directly reached by the program *(caregivers gain knowledge, change behavior)*
- **DISTAL OUTCOME:** Change in the group ultimately targeted *(children show improved development)*

> **THE INDIRECT PATHWAY**
> Program → Caregiver (direct) → Child (distal)
>
> The program does NOT directly develop children's brains. It develops caregivers' capacity to develop children's brains. This is why caregiver behavioral change is the critical mechanism in this Theory of Change.

#### Slide 10 — Monitoring and evaluation: different questions, shared theory
**⏱ 5 min**

**Purpose**
Briefly introduce the M&E distinction. Both anchor in the same results chain. A conceptual seed — developed fully in Block 5.

**Content on slide**

| MONITORING | EVALUATION |
|---|---|
| Are we delivering as planned? | Are outcomes occurring as expected? |
| Tracks outputs and early outcomes over time | Tests whether the ToC holds — and why |
| Signals early warning: is the theory unfolding? | Assesses the strength of the contribution claim |
| Continuous — during implementation | Periodic — at key milestones |

**Key message**
> *"Both M&E use the same Theory of Change. That is why building a good ToC today is foundational to everything that follows this week."*

### BLOCK 3 — THEORY OF CHANGE
*~70 minutes · Slides 14–21*

---

#### Slide 14 — What is a Theory of Change?
**⏱ 8 min**

**Purpose**
Define Theory of Change precisely. Distinguish from logic model. Frame it as an argument, not a diagram.

> **DEFINITION**
> A Theory of Change is a causal argument that explains how and why an intervention is expected to produce change. It specifies: (1) the results chain, (2) the causal mechanisms, (3) the causal assumptions, and (4) external influences.

**What makes it different from a logic model**
- A logic model describes; a ToC argues
- A logic model says "if we do X, then Y"; a ToC says "if we do X, then Y, BECAUSE OF Z, assuming A and B hold"
- A ToC makes the mechanism and assumptions explicit — and therefore testable

**Key reference**
> Rogers (2008); Koleros & Mayne (2017): Actor-based ToCs make clear whose behavior must change, and under what conditions.

---

#### Slide 15 — What is missing from the results chain?
**⏱ 7 min**

**Content on slide**

*Results chain says:* Sessions delivered → Caregiver behavior change → Child development

*But does NOT explain:*
- WHY would a session change caregiver behavior? → **Causal mechanism**
- WHAT must be true for that change to happen? → **Causal assumptions**
- WHAT else could produce the observed outcome? → **External influences**

**Key teaching point**
> *"The jump from sessions delivered to child development improved is not self-evident. It is a theory. And like any theory, it can be wrong."*

---

#### Slide 16 — Causal mechanisms: how does change actually happen?
**⏱ 8 min**

**Purpose**
Introduce mechanism as the engine of the ToC. Mechanisms connect outputs to outcomes.

**Key distinctions**
- A causal mechanism is the process through which an output produces an outcome
- Mechanisms are not activities — activities produce outputs; mechanisms explain how outputs produce outcomes
- In theory-based evaluation, we test mechanisms directly — not just outcomes

> **EXAMPLE: CRECIENDO JUNTOS**
>
> Activity: 36 parenting group sessions delivered
> Mechanism: Sessions increase caregiver **CAPABILITY** (knowledge of child development), create **OPPORTUNITY** (peer networks, materials), and build **MOTIVATION** (new identity as a parent)
> Outcome: Caregivers change daily interaction behaviors (more responsive, language-rich, nurturing)
> Distal outcome: Children experience improved stimulation → developmental gains

**Key teaching point**
> *"The mechanism is what sessions are supposed to trigger in caregivers. If the mechanism does not fire, sessions may be delivered but nothing changes."*

---

#### Slide 17 — COM-B: capability, opportunity, motivation → behavior
**⏱ 8 min**

**Purpose**
Introduce COM-B as a structured lens for analyzing behavioral mechanisms. Use as a thinking tool, not a rigid framework.

| Component | Meaning | In Creciendo Juntos |
|---|---|---|
| **CAPABILITY (C)** | What a person knows and can do | Caregiver learns developmental milestones, learns to play responsively |
| **OPPORTUNITY (O)** | What the environment enables or constrains | Peer group forms, materials distributed, health visits accessible |
| **MOTIVATION (M)** | What drives action | Parent sees child respond positively; new identity: "I am my child's first teacher" |
| **→ BEHAVIOR** | Observable action that produces the outcome | Daily responsive interaction increases from 15 → 45 min; harsh discipline replaced |

**Facilitator note**
> COM-B is an analytical lens, not a checklist. Its value is forcing the question: what exactly needs to shift in the person for new behavior to become possible?

---

**⏱ 5 min**

**Purpose**
Apply COM-B to the case study before the exercise. Show a partially completed COM-B analysis as a model.

*CJ sessions → [C: What capability?] + [O: What opportunity?] + [M: What motivation?] → Behavior change → Child development*

- **Capability:** Knowledge of child development stages · Skill in responsive interaction · Self-efficacy
- **Opportunity:** Peer support group · Learning materials · Health center access
- **Motivation:** Emotional reward from child's positive response · Peer norms · New parental identity

### BLOCK 4 — ASSUMPTIONS AND EXTERNAL INFLUENCES
*~65 minutes · Slides 22–29*

---

#### Slide 22 — What are causal assumptions?
**⏱ 7 min**

> **DEFINITION: CAUSAL ASSUMPTIONS**
>
> A causal assumption is a condition that must hold for one level of the results chain to produce the next. If an assumption is violated, the expected causal link breaks — even if the program is implemented correctly. Assumptions are explicit acknowledgments of the conditions on which the theory depends.

**Example from Creciendo Juntos**
- Link: Parenting sessions delivered → Caregivers change behavior
- Assumption: *"Attendance is sufficient to build capability and motivation (caregivers attend ≥ 70% of sessions)"*
- If violated: Sessions delivered but caregivers attend sporadically → capability does not build → behavior does not change → outcomes not reached — even though outputs were 100% delivered

**Key teaching point**
> *"This is why monitoring attendance is not optional. If this assumption breaks, everything downstream breaks with it."*

---

#### Slide 23 — Types of assumptions: classifying by results chain level
**⏱ 8 min**

| Assumption type | Question it answers | CJ example |
|---|---|---|
| **REACH** | Can the program reach the intended population? | Facilitators can be recruited and retained in target communities |
| **CAPACITY** | Does the intervention build the required COM-B shift? | Sessions are delivered with sufficient quality to build caregiver capability |
| **BEHAVIOR** | Does changed COM-B actually produce behavior change? | Increased knowledge and motivation translate to daily behavior change (not just during sessions) |
| **OUTCOME** | Does behavior change produce the expected outcome? | Changed caregiver behavior is sustained for 18+ months and produces developmental gains |

---

#### Slide 24 — Critical assumptions: which links are most fragile?
**⏱ 5 min**

A critical assumption is one that:
1. Is **essential** — if it breaks, outcomes cannot be reached regardless of delivery quality
2. Is **uncertain** — we have limited evidence that it holds in this context
3. Is **testable** — we can design monitoring or evaluation to check it

> **CJ EXAMPLE: TWO CANDIDATE CRITICAL ASSUMPTIONS**
>
> **Assumption B1 (BEHAVIOR):** "Caregivers who increase knowledge in sessions also change their daily parenting behaviors at home, not just during observed sessions." — CRITICAL because behavioral generalization is uncertain and rarely measured.
>
> **Assumption O1 (OUTCOME):** "Changed parenting behavior, sustained for 18 months, is sufficient to produce measurable developmental gains in children by age 3." — CRITICAL because the dose-response relationship is unclear for multi-risk households.

#### Slide 27 — External influences: what is outside the program's control?
**⏱ 7 min**

**Two types of external influence:**

**CONTEXTUAL FACTORS** — Structural conditions that shape whether the mechanism can function (economy, political stability, health system quality)

**RIVAL EXPLANATIONS** — Alternative causes of the observed outcome that would have occurred without the program (natural development, another program, a social trend)

> **WHY BOTH MATTER FOR EVALUATION**
>
> Contextual factors can violate assumptions — e.g., severe food insecurity may mean that even improved parenting behavior does not translate to developmental gains.
>
> Rival explanations: if children improve even in control communities, the program's contribution is less clear. Evaluators must rule out rival explanations or account for them in the contribution claim.

- Evaluation asks: Did expected outcomes occur? Did the theory's mechanisms function as hypothesized?
- Contribution analysis (Session 5): *"Given what we observe, how strong is our claim that the program contributed to these outcomes?"*
- The ToC built today is the foundation of the contribution claim — every assumption identified is a piece of evidence to test

**Thread to Sessions 3–5**
> *"Sessions 3 and 4 will ask: what qualitative and quantitative evidence would let you test whether each mechanism and assumption held? Session 5 brings all of that evidence into a contribution claim. Today's ToC is the map."*
